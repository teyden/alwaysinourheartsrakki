The story of how Rakki came into my life, our relationship, and his relationship with the world around him is a unique one. It is rare that someone in their early twenties, in university, and with no real stability would undertake the responsibility of having a dog. However, upon first meeting Rakki, it was undeniable that in the circumstance that he was in need, I would step up and become his primary caregiver. I was 21 when I met him at a mere 3-months old, and not long after did I take over full responsibility and adopted him as my own. The next 9 years involved moving from university home to university home as I finished up my degree, started a new job, then moved across the country to pursue graduate studies. Rakki was alongside the entire way, on every road-trip, adventure, late-night study session, social gathering, and family dinner. He was my other half. Having a dog in my early twenties while not settled down in life was not easy. The help from close friends who absolutely loved Rakki was thoroughly needed, and this — defined much of his and my life. Since I was a student for a lot of it, there were always periods where I needed dog-sitting help and very fortunately, there was always a long line-up of friends ready to pitch in by taking him for a few days to a few weeks (sometimes a few months if I was traveling). He was a popular dog that was well socialized and adapted to new environments easily. I was a lucky pet guardian. Rakki was my first dog love. And I am not the only one who experienced this with him. Rakki had the ability to connect with people whether or not they were dog-lovers. One of his special talents was “wooing” non-animal people, including those who were previously fearful of dogs. He was a therapist at heart - he knew how to console people when they were sad, and he knew how to melt your heart. More than a number of times, he had been dog-sat by a friend of mine who lived with parents who weren’t a fan of dogs. Each and every one of these times, he left their homes having “turned” the parents into dog-lovers. Or, at the very least, “Rakki-lovers”. I have always been amazed at how Rakki was always able to “turn” people and build such a large fan-base. He had more friends than I did, and I had often met people who knew my dog but did not know me. “Hey, YOU are Rakki’s owner?! Oh my god, he is AWEsome!”. This would always make me laugh. Rakki lived an adventurous lifestyle. He was a mere 10 lbs of fluff but with the character and impact of a human. He was sensitive and compassionate, sweet yet bold, fierce, and independent. He loved his hour-plus long walks, intense 9-hour hikes, belly rubs and long cuddle sessions. He was the dog of my dreams. He followed me through my lifestyle of “work hard and play harder”. We adventured together and traveled from coast to coast, hiking and backpacking mountains in Newfoundland, Ontario, and BC. When moving for graduate school, I packed my car with all of my belongings and drove cross-country through the US to get to Vancouver from Toronto. Rakki slept above all of my “crap” in the little loft bedroom I set up for him in the back. Whether we were in Detroit, Chicago, Minneapolis, in some random town in Wyoming, Yellowstone National Park, or Seattle — we felt safe because we were together. Now back in my childhood home — Vancouver, Rakki had a new family. My sister and my best friends. A month-long trip for me overseas involved a 4-person discussion (let’s be real, it was a debate) on who gets custody of Rakki on which week that I was away. With my new lifestyle as a graduate student, I spent much of the 2.5 years in Vancouver working in my version of “solitude” — alone but never really lonely; because I had Rakki. Fast-forward to 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic began. May of that year, Rakki was unfortunately diagnosed with a heart valve condition that would cut down his lifespan by a number of years. At 9 years of age, with a fairly active lifestyle and being fed what I thought was the best diet for dogs over the years (grain-free foods), this was a tragedy as we truly believed he had a lot more time left. Accepting that he had an estimated 9 months left to live was devastating for us all. The one good thing that came from this global pandemic was the amount of time I was able to stay home with him — to watch him, care for him, and spend time with him. It was a blessing in disguise. After countless hours of educating myself on his condition and how best to care for him, there were many weeks where we got to see a joyful, healthy Rakki run around like he did prior to becoming ill. *How good he was during this time. Still thought he was a baby* Nearly 6 months later, he unfortunately did not meet nor beat the statistic and other issues that can result from heart conditions caught up to him. Within a quick 36 hours, he was found to have lost sensation in one, then two legs, leaving him paralyzed and at risk of amputation of both legs. With his heart condition, there was a very low chance he would survive the surgery — and even if he did, he wouldn’t be able to live a good quality of life. Rakki passed away peacefully on the afternoon of October 27th, 2020. He was a little angel sent to earth that will forever be remembered in all of our hearts.

Dear Rakki,

I still remember meeting you for the first time in University when you were just a puppy, you were running around non-stop as if you had one too many Red Bulls. I wasn't very fond of you at first because you were such a little shit disturber. I guess you sensed my displeasure so you decided to sit beside me and use those puppy eyes to break the ice. After I reciprocated you jumped straight into my arms and hugged me as if we were old friends. I had such a good time playing with you that night I even took photos to remember that moment.

After that encounter, I knew that you were a special dog, you understood human emotions, you felt everyone's energy and you were loyal to those that took care of you. You were full of positivity and all you wanted was to make everyone around you happy. This is also the reason I asked to dog-sit you because my mother was never a pet owner and she wasn't fond of me getting a dog. I was very nervous before taking you home because I didn't ask for my mom's approval. I even made up a whole story about how you were found in a dumpster to score sympathy points, thinking this would make the process smoother.

It took you just a few minutes to break down my mother's barrier and win her with your charm. Within a few weeks, my childhood pictures started getting replaced by your photos, you brought out a side of my mother that I've never seen before.

You changed her life and you also changed mine.

You were the first dog that I bonded with, you helped me understand the connection that dogs and humans can share. You gave me the courage to get my own dog as I got older and now I've dedicated my life creating pet products for dogs and their humans.

Thank you for leading me on this path, your spirit and energy will always be part of our brand and be reflected in our work.

Love,

— Neo Yin
